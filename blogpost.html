Arizona's National Parks

The real voyage of discovery consists not in seeking new landscapes, having new eyes.

Grand Canyon

Grand Canyon's Flaming Gorge got its name for its blazing red and orange colored, twelve-hundred-foot-high walls. Grand Canyon's Disaster Falls was named to commemorate the site of a previous explorer's wreck. Grand Canyon's Marble Canyon got its name from its thousand-foot-thick seam of marble and for its walls eroded to a polished glass finish.

Chiricahua

Chiricahua National Monument is a Hidden Gem that sees less than 60,000 visitors a year. It’s simply one of Arizona’s best-kept secrets.

Kartchner Caverns

The Public Finally Learned of the Kartchner Caverns in 1988 discovered in 1984 by Gary Tenen and Randy Tufts. The formation in the Caverns are called Speleothems. They form when water passes from the surface into the cave. The minerals in the cave mixed with the water over time form these amazing and archetypal giant cave structures.

Petrified Forest

The Petrified Forest is the only national park site that contains a segment of the Historic Route 66 alignment. The brilliant colors in the petrified wood come mainly from three minerals. Pure quartz is white, manganese oxides form blue, purple, black, and brown, and iron oxides provide hues from yellow through red to brown. Petrified wood is the state fossil.

Colossal Cave

The Colossal Cave was formed during the Mississippian Period. When water seeped through the rocks and dissolved away part of the limestone. It's the third largest cave and one of the 5% of dry/dormant caves in the world. Which means it's still alive, but no longer growing. It makes it one of the rare caves where pictures can be taken inside. The cave stays an even 70 year round.
